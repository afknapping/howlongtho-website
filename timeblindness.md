<h1>Time blindness, explained</h1>
<p class="lead">You know that feeling where five minutes and forty-five minutes feel exactly the same — right up until one of them turns out to be very, very late? That's not carelessness. It has a name.</p>
<h2>What it actually is</h2>
<p>Most people carry a rough sense of time passing in the background, even when they're not looking at a clock. Time blindness means that background sense doesn't reliably work. Minutes don't quietly add up into a felt sense of "it's later than I think" — until the deadline is already here.</p>
<p>It's common in ADHD, tied to how the brain handles time passing in the background. But you don't need a diagnosis to recognize it. Plenty of people who've never been evaluated for anything have spent their whole life wondering why time seems to work differently for them than it does for everyone else.</p>
<h2>Where it shows up</h2>
<ul class="features">
<li>Losing a whole afternoon to something absorbing, and surfacing stunned at how late it already is</li>
<li>Feeling sure you have "plenty of time" — right up until you very much don't</li>
<li>Knowing a deadline is coming, but not feeling it coming, until it's suddenly right on top of you</li>
<li>Being late — not from not caring, but from consistently underestimating how long things actually take</li>
</ul>
<p>It shows up at work too. People who experience this are measurably more likely to lose jobs and to earn less over their careers — not because they work less hard, but because the clock everyone else seems to carry automatically, they have to build for themselves, from scratch, every single day.</p>
<h2>What it leaves behind</h2>
<p>The lateness itself usually isn't the real cost. It's what gets built on top of it, one incident at a time, over years.</p>
<p>Kids who experience this hear a lot of "hurry up," "why didn't you," "you always." Not cruelty — just ordinary correction, repeated thousands of times over a childhood. Repeated enough, it stops sounding like "you did something wrong" and starts sounding like "you are something wrong."</p>
<p>As an adult, it turns quieter: meaning it, every single time, and still not making it happen. Eventually the hardest person left to convince isn't anyone else. It's yourself, the next time you promise you'll be there.</p>
<p>Friendships rarely end over one big moment. They erode — a late reply here, a canceled plan there, each one too small to name — until the invitations quietly stop coming, and the loneliness that's left doesn't even come with a clear reason attached.</p>
<p>And because none of it ever really resets, each new slip doesn't feel like one bad day. It feels like the newest piece of evidence in a case that was already decided a long time ago.</p>
<p class="lead">None of that is a character flaw. It's what happens when a real difference in how a brain works gets treated, over and over, like a decision someone keeps making on purpose.</p>
<h2>The coping habits nobody clocks as coping</h2>
<p>A brain that's chasing a feeling it doesn't reliably get — enough stimulation, enough relief, enough "right now" — will usually find it somewhere. Sometimes that's a cigarette, a late-night scroll, a bet, a binge, a purchase that felt necessary at 11pm and baffling by morning. It's the same mechanism as the lateness itself: a fast, real answer from a brain that isn't getting what it needs the slow way. Not a separate weakness stacked on top of the first one — the same thing, wearing a different coat.</p>
<h2>What actually helps</h2>
<p>Understanding the mechanism tends to be the first real relief people describe — not because it fixes anything by itself, but because it turns "what's wrong with me" into "oh, that's what's happening." From there, real things help: some people find medication or therapy makes a measurable difference; others find that simply making time visible — an external clock standing in for an internal one that doesn't reliably fire — is enough to change how a day actually goes. That's the whole idea behind How Long Tho: not a fix, just a second, more honest clock.</p>
<p>If none of this is your experience — if you've never lost track of an afternoon or been blindsided by a deadline you technically knew was coming — it's still worth having read this far. The people in your life who do experience it aren't choosing to let you down. They're running on a clock that was never built to warn them in time. A little patience goes further than a reminder that they're late again. They already know.</p>
